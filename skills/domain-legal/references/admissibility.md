# Admissibility & Expert Considerations (domain-specific)

> **Status: methodology framework, NOT legal advice.** Confirm requirements with counsel for the jurisdiction and current case law.

## The defensibility checklist (universal)
- [ ] **Universe** — the relevant consumer population correctly defined and screened (top reason surveys are excluded).
- [ ] **Control** — control cell/stimulus to net out noise and guessing.
- [ ] **Double-blind** — interviewer and respondent blind to sponsor and purpose.
- [ ] **Non-leading** — no suggestive wording; pretest for it.
- [ ] **Stimuli** — realistic, neutral presentation of marks/products.
- [ ] **Documentation & chain of custody** — everything reproducible; expert deposable.
- [ ] **Neutrality** — expert is not an advocate; disclose methods and limitations.

## US framing
Reliability under Daubert / FRE 702; the Reference Manual on Scientific Evidence (survey chapter), McCarthy, and the Manual for Complex Litigation inform expectations. Recognized formats in `survey-formats.md`.

## Russia framing — SIP information note (authoritative)
The Presidium of the Court for Intellectual Property Rights (Суд по интеллектуальным правам) approved an information note on how courts evaluate consumer-opinion surveys in trademark disputes (постановление президиума СИП от 18.08.2022 № СП-21/15). It is an official state-body document. The court asks, overall: **is the methodology fit for the task?** Synthesized criteria:

### Conducting organization & general requirements
The survey is one piece of evidence, weighed with the rest. The report must disclose the organization, the methodology, the universe, the questions, and the stimulus materials, plus interviewer instructions — enough that the court (and opposing experts) can check it.

### Universe & sample (круг опрошенных лиц)
- **Target audience (целевая аудитория):** all potential consumers relevant to the question, narrowed by the mark and the goods/services (age, sex, profession, geography). The standard is the **average consumer (рядовой, средний потребитель)** addressee of the goods *in the Russian Federation*.
- **Representativeness:** the sample must represent the target audience within statistical error. Because reliable data on the general population is often absent, structural representativeness usually can't be proven directly; practitioners instead control three things: (1) socio-demographic and geographic diversity (don't let the sample be homogeneous by age/sex/education/income), (2) randomness of selection (at least at the final stage), (3) sample size giving 95% confidence with a margin of error from 5% (≈400 people) to 2.5% (≈1600).
- **Size:** typically **400 (min) to ~2000 (max)**, varying by case; count alone doesn't make a sample representative but must be statistically meaningful. EU IPO/CJEU guidance cited as a reference point: 1000–2000 for mass-consumption goods; lower for specialized/concentrated markets; treat <300 with caution and <100 as unreliable absent a reasoned explanation.
- **Geography:** distribution across the RF matters. Surveys confined to a few cities of one federal district have been rejected (e.g., 800 respondents in 5 cities of the Southern district; 98 in a single city). Rospatent Recommendations give an *orientational* benchmark (~500 in each of two large cities + ~125 in each additional city) — not a rigid rule; the minimum varies with how narrow/broad the consumer circle is.
- **Screening (отборочные/скрининговые вопросы):** use them to admit only the relevant audience, without pre-announcing the survey's subject. Distinguish durable vs. everyday goods — capture *fact* of purchase for durables, *frequency* for everyday goods (to exclude incidental buyers).

### Question wording (формулировка вопросов)
- Objective, neutral, simple, accessible. One subject per question — **no double-barreled** items; no ambiguity; don't force the respondent to supply missing context.
- **No semantic shortcuts:** e.g., don't ask "are these designations the same or not?" and treat "no" as proof of no similarity.
- **No leading/oriented questions** (наводящие вопросы); don't exploit the consistency principle or other persuasion techniques; avoid wording that touches the respondent's self-esteem ("would you make a mistake if you saw these two on the shelf?").
- **Open before aided:** open/unaided questions carry the highest probative weight (spontaneous naming); aided/closed questions depend on exact wording and options. Recommended sequence: (1) open unaided naming tied to the product category, (2) aided recognition via a list, (3) **control questions** to confirm answers weren't externally influenced. Empirical rule: the more spontaneity/difficulty a correct answer requires, the more reliable it is.
- **Complexity:** not too hard to parse; avoid double negatives; watch for questions that are secretly two questions.
- **Abbreviations & terms:** spell out abbreviations; keep wording close to legal language ("face validity") but not purely legal; explain special terms or give examples; be careful with phrases like "related companies."
- **Stimulus materials (сопутствующие материалы):** neutrality applies to all shown materials. Images undistorted; handouts neutral with no leading elements; presentation order must not influence answers; when comparing two marks, separate them so each is perceived independently; show the actual element at issue (not packaging carrying extra words/images).

### Subject of the survey (предмет опроса)
- **The designation (обозначение):** survey the mark *as registered or applied for*. A survey of a different mark is irrelevant evidence unless consumers perceive the two as the same.
- **Goods/services:** conduct it for the goods/services the mark is registered/applied for; tie perception to the goods, not the mark in the abstract (a survey that fixes attention on the marks "without attachment to the marked goods" doesn't establish actual confusion).
- **Temporal parameters:** results must let the court establish facts as of the legally relevant date. Retrospective surveys get extra scrutiny — align questions to the relevant date(s) and assess the gap between fieldwork date and the date at issue.
